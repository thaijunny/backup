Annotated Bibliography (MLA Format)
Junco, Reynol. “Too Much Face and Not Enough Books: The Relationship Between Multiple Indices of Facebook Use and Academic Performance.” Computers in Human Behavior, vol. 28, no. 1, 2012, pp. 187–198.

This article examines the relationship between Facebook use and academic performance among college students. The author collected data through surveys measuring students’ time spent on Facebook, types of activities, and their GPA. The study found that frequent Facebook use for non-academic purposes negatively affects academic performance, while academic-related use may be beneficial. This source is credible because it is published in a peer-reviewed academic journal. It is useful for this project because it provides clear evidence of how different types of social media use can influence students’ academic success.

Karpinski, Aryn C., et al. “An Exploration of Social Networking Site Use, Multitasking, and Academic Performance Among United States and European University Students.” Computers in Human Behavior, vol. 29, no. 3, 2013, pp. 1182–1192.

This study explores how social networking and multitasking affect academic performance among university students in the United States and Europe. The researchers used questionnaires to collect data on students’ social media habits, multitasking behavior, and academic outcomes. The results show that students who frequently multitask with social media tend to have lower academic performance. This source is academic and reliable because it uses empirical data and comparative analysis. It is valuable for this research because it highlights the negative effects of multitasking with social media on learning.

Paul, Joshua A., Heather M. Baker, and Jeffrey D. Cochran. “Effect of Online Social Networking on Student Academic Performance.” Computers in Human Behavior, vol. 28, no. 6, 2012, pp. 2117–2127.

This article investigates the impact of online social networking on students’ academic performance. The authors conducted a quantitative study using survey data from college students to analyze time spent on social networking sites and study habits. The findings indicate that excessive use of social networking sites reduces time devoted to academic activities, leading to lower grades. This source is academic because it is published in a scholarly journal and follows a scientific research method. It supports the project by reinforcing the argument that uncontrolled social media use can negatively affect students’ academic outcomes.
